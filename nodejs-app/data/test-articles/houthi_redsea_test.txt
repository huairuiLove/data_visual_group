Title: Houthis Escalate Red Sea Attacks, Targeting US Navy Carrier Group
Date: 2025-04-22
Source: Associated Press

Summary:
Yemen's Houthi rebels intensify missile and drone attacks against US naval vessels and commercial shipping in the Red Sea, disrupting global trade routes.

Article:
SANAA — Houthi forces launched a coordinated missile and drone attack against the USS Harry S. Truman carrier strike group in the Red Sea on Monday, marking the most significant escalation in the maritime conflict since November 2023.

The Iran-backed Houthi movement, which controls much of northern Yemen including the capital Sanaa, claimed the attack using "winged missiles and drones" targeted the aircraft carrier and several escort vessels. US Central Command (CENTCOM) confirmed the attack but said all projectiles were intercepted by naval air defense systems without damage or casualties.

Houthi military spokesman Yahya Saree said the operation was in retaliation for "American-British aggression against our country" and in support of "the oppressed Palestinian people in Gaza." He warned that Red Sea and Arabian Sea shipping lanes would remain closed to "Israeli, American, and British vessels" until the siege on Gaza is lifted.

The attack follows a pattern of escalating Houthi strikes against commercial and military vessels in the critical waterway through which approximately 12% of global trade passes. Since October 2023, the Houthis have attacked more than 150 ships, seized one vessel, sunk two, and killed four sailors.

Major shipping companies including Maersk, CMA CGM, and Hapag-Lloyd continue to reroute vessels around Africa's Cape of Good Hope, adding 10-14 days of transit time and approximately $1 million in additional fuel costs per voyage. Insurance premiums for Red Sea transits have risen by 400% since the crisis began.

Egypt's Suez Canal Authority reported a 60% decline in revenue over the past 18 months, costing the Egyptian economy an estimated $7 billion in lost canal fees. The economic impact has been particularly severe given Egypt's ongoing currency crisis and high inflation.

In response, US and UK forces have conducted multiple rounds of airstrikes against Houthi military targets in Yemen since January 2024, including missile launch sites, radar installations, weapons storage facilities, and drone manufacturing workshops. Despite these strikes, the Houthis have continued and arguably accelerated their attacks.

CENTCOM Commander General Michael Kurilla told Congress last week that the Houthis have received "unprecedented levels" of technical support from Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC), including intelligence from the Iranian spy ship MV Behshad operating in the Gulf of Aden. Iran has denied direct involvement in Houthi operations.

The European Union's naval mission in the Red Sea, Operation Aspides, has escorted hundreds of merchant vessels through the danger zone but lacks the offensive capability to strike Houthi launch sites ashore. France, Italy, and Germany have deployed frigates to the region.

Houthi leader Abdul-Malik al-Houthi said in a televised address that the group has prepared "500,000 trained fighters" for a potential ground confrontation and unveiled new domestically manufactured hypersonic missiles, though military analysts question these claims.

The United Nations Special Envoy to Yemen, Hans Grundberg, warned that the Red Sea escalation has "effectively frozen" the Yemen peace process, which had shown progress before October 2023. Saudi Arabia, which had been negotiating a ceasefire with the Houthis, suspended talks indefinitely.

Oil prices have remained volatile, with Brent crude trading at $92 per barrel — up 15% since the Red Sea crisis began. The International Energy Agency warned that prolonged disruption could push oil above $120 per barrel and add 0.5% to global inflation.
