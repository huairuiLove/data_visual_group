Title: Iran-Backed Iraqi Militias Launch Drone Strikes on US Base in Syria
Date: 2025-06-02
Source: BBC News

Summary:
Iran-backed Iraqi militias escalate attacks on US forces in Syria and Iraq, complicating US troop presence and regional security dynamics.

Article:
DAMASCUS — Iranian-backed Iraqi militia groups launched a coordinated one-way drone attack against the US military base at Al-Tanf in southeastern Syria on Sunday, injuring five American service members and destroying a fuel depot.

The Islamic Resistance in Iraq, an umbrella group of Shia militias including Kata'ib Hezbollah, Harakat al-Nujaba, and Asa'ib Ahl al-Haq, claimed responsibility for the attack. The group said it was retaliation for US support of Israeli military operations and the continued American military presence in Iraq and Syria.

US forces at Al-Tanf, which sits at a strategically important border crossing between Syria and Iraq near Jordan, responded with counter-battery fire targeting militia launch sites. CENTCOM confirmed the base was "fully operational" within hours and said the injured personnel were in stable condition.

The attack came amid a broader escalation pattern. Since January 2025, Iran-backed militias have launched over 180 attacks against US forces in Iraq and Syria, including rocket attacks on Ain al-Asad airbase in western Iraq and the US Embassy compound in Baghdad's Green Zone.

Iraqi Prime Minister Mohammed Shia al-Sudani condemned the attacks and ordered security forces to pursue those responsible, saying "attacks on diplomatic missions and international coalition bases are terrorist acts that undermine Iraq's sovereignty." However, the Iraqi government's ability to control these militias — many of which are formally part of Iraq's Popular Mobilization Forces (PMF) and receive state salaries — remains limited.

The US maintains approximately 2,500 troops in Iraq and 900 in Syria as part of the international coalition against ISIS. While the military mission is officially focused on counterterrorism operations, Iran and its allies view the US presence as a permanent occupation.

Syria's Foreign Ministry condemned what it called "illegal US military presence" in its territory, while Russia, which maintains its own military bases in Syria, called for "restraint from all parties" in the UN Security Council.

Israel has separately carried out hundreds of airstrikes in Syria targeting Iranian weapons shipments, IRGC positions, and Hezbollah supply routes. An Israeli airstrike on a convoy near the Iraq-Syria border last month killed two IRGC officers and destroyed a shipment of precision-guided munitions components.

Jordan, which hosts additional US forces and shares borders with both Syria and Iraq, is increasingly concerned about spillover violence. Jordanian Foreign Minister Ayman Safadi called for an "urgent international mechanism" to monitor and prevent militia activities along the Jordan-Syria-Iraq border triangle.

The United Nations reported that Iran-backed militias in Iraq have expanded their influence significantly since the defeat of ISIS, now controlling numerous front companies in construction, oil smuggling, and cross-border trade worth an estimated $3 billion annually. These revenue streams, combined with Iranian financial support, have enabled militias to acquire advanced drone technology and precision-guided weapons.

Analysts at the Washington Institute for Near East Policy noted that the militias are increasingly operating as a "coordinated network" under IRGC Quds Force direction, with operational coordination between Kata'ib Hezbollah in Iraq, Hezbollah in Lebanon, and Houthi forces in Yemen creating what they termed a "Shia corridor of asymmetric power projection."

The US and Iraq are currently negotiating a timetable for the withdrawal of coalition forces, though the talks have been complicated by the recent escalation. Washington seeks a "conditions-based" timeline tied to Iraqi security forces' capability, while Baghdad — under pressure from Iran-aligned political blocs — demands a fixed deadline.
